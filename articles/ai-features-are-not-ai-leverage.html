Product thinking

AI Features Are Not the Same as AI Leverage

I am starting to think the interesting question is not only how to add AI to existing software. It is what kind of harness a neural computer actually needs.

Computers were mostly designed, at least in the way most of us use them, like very fast calculators. We give them explicit inputs. They follow workflows. They store artifacts. They move information between files, fields, rows, folders, tickets, approvals, messages, and dashboards. The software asks us to translate messy human intent into forms the machine can understand.

This model has worked incredibly well. It gave us spreadsheets, databases, messaging systems, project trackers, document editors, workflow engines, and most of the coordination infrastructure of modern work. It also shaped our instincts about software. A product has screens. A screen has controls. A control changes a record. A record belongs somewhere. A workflow advances through states.

Modern AI models make the computer feel more neural. Not perfectly intelligent. Not magical. But different. A model can reason in language, infer intent, inspect context, critique output, generate plans, call tools, and sometimes act across a task. It can move from a vague request toward a structured artifact without the user having to specify every step.

The mismatch is that most software around these models is still calculator-era software. We put neural capability into systems organized around messages, files, fields, buttons, tickets, docs, meetings, approvals, and static workflows. The model may be flexible, but the harness around it often assumes the old unit of work.

A powerful model placed inside old software can feel like a neural computer trapped inside calculator-era workflows. I do not mean that as a complaint about the old workflows. They are old because they are useful. They create legibility, auditability, ownership, and shared memory. But I suspect the product question is shifting. The question is not just "Where can we add an AI feature?" It is "What is the right harness for a neural computer?"

AI convenience versus AI leverage

A lot of AI product work today creates real convenience. I do not want to dismiss that. Convenience matters. Shortening a search, improving a draft, summarizing a thread, or turning notes into a checklist can make a day noticeably better.

But convenience is not the same as leverage. Convenience makes an existing loop smoother. Leverage may change where the loop should exist at all.

The distinction I am starting to use is between three levels of AI product value. The boundaries are not clean, and a product can move across levels. But the categories help me understand why some AI features feel useful without feeling like they change the underlying work system.

The first level is the Q&A machine. It reduces the cost of knowing. The second level is the agentic task assistant. It reduces the cost of doing small tasks. The third level is a high-leverage harness. It helps identify the smallest human judgment that creates the largest net-positive outcome.

Q&A machines reduce the cost of knowing

The Q&A machine is often the first place AI becomes useful in a work product. It answers questions like: "What did this person say about this topic?" "When is my next meeting with this team?" "What is this policy?" "Where is the latest doc?" "What did we decide last time?" "Who owns this?"

These questions are not trivial. Knowing is expensive in modern work. Context is fragmented across messages, meetings, docs, tickets, comments, approvals, and people's memories. Much of work is really reconstruction work: finding the latest state, understanding how we got here, remembering who said what, and locating the source of truth.

Reducing the cost of knowing is valuable because it lowers the cost of orientation. A Q&A system can help someone join a project, catch up after time away, understand an escalation, or find the artifact they need without asking three people for help.

But I suspect Q&A usually does not create 10X or 100X leverage by itself. It helps the user understand the existing state. It does not necessarily change the state. It makes the old information system more searchable, which is useful, but the work graph underneath may remain just as tangled as before.

The risk is that we confuse better retrieval with better work. A person can find the answer faster and still be stuck in the same decision loop, the same approval loop, the same handoff problem, or the same ambiguous ownership boundary.

Task assistants reduce the cost of small actions

The second level is the agentic task assistant. It does things like triage a queue, draft a reply, batch approve low-risk expenses, make a message sound better, fix grammar, summarize a thread, or create tickets from meeting notes.

These assistants can be genuinely useful. There is a lot of repetitive work in knowledge work. A system that removes small irritations can create real relief. If it drafts the message, cleans up the note, creates the ticket, and reminds the right person, that is a better experience than making the user do every mechanical step.

Still, many of these assistants mostly make existing workflows smoother. The message remains a message. The ticket remains a ticket. The approval remains an approval. The doc remains a doc. The human still lives inside the same loop, only with a better backspace key and a faster clerk.

That can be worth building. But it may not be a fundamentally new work system. The system is faster, but the shape of the work is mostly unchanged. The AI helps the user push the old objects around more efficiently.

This is where I think the phrase "agent" can become a little misleading. If an agent just performs more small actions inside an old workflow, it may be helpful without being high leverage. The product question is not only "Can the agent act?" It is "Is this the action that matters?"

Not more motion. Better consequence.

The framing I keep resisting is "largest amount of work." It is easy to imagine a great AI system as one that does the most work for us: more emails sent, more docs created, more meetings recorded, more tickets closed, more code shipped, more approvals processed, and more people unblocked.

But more work can simply mean more motion. And more motion can be dangerous.

A system that optimizes for motion can look productive while making the organization less effective. It can unblock people in the wrong direction. It can generate documents that create false alignment. It can close tickets without resolving the underlying issue. It can produce status updates that make coordination feel complete when the real decision is still unresolved.

The question I keep coming back to is:

Where is the smallest human judgment that creates the largest net-positive outcome?

"Net-positive" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. It does not mean more activity. It includes customer trust, quality, speed, morale, risk, opportunity cost, future maintenance burden, precedent, coordination cost, and whether the organization is moving in the right direction.

This framing changes the product shape. The goal is not to automate the most visible activity. The goal is to find the moment where a small amount of human judgment, applied with the right context, changes the trajectory of the work.

The model is productive force. The harness is production relation.

There is a phrase from Chinese that keeps coming to mind, although I would use it carefully: sheng chan li, 生产力, means productive force. Sheng chan guan xi, 生产关系, means production relations.

I am not trying to turn this into a political theory essay. The practical product idea is simpler. The model may be a new productive force. It can do new kinds of cognitive work, or at least make certain cognitive operations much cheaper. But the harness changes the production relations around that capability: who decides, what context is visible, which tradeoffs are considered, what the system is allowed to do, how decisions propagate, and how outcomes are judged later.

If the harness is just a prompt box bolted onto old software, the model's capability gets expressed through the old units of work. It answers questions, drafts messages, edits docs, files tickets, and moves records. Those are useful expressions of the model. But maybe they are not the highest-leverage expression.

A high-leverage harness would look for judgment points. It would ask: where is the decision that changes the work graph? Where is the ambiguous ownership boundary? Where is repeated work indicating a missing policy? Where are two teams using the same words but meaning different things? Where does a small human call prevent a large amount of downstream waste?

The harness needs a work graph, not just a prompt box

A prompt box is a powerful interface because it is open-ended. But open-endedness is not the same as context. If the system does not understand the work graph, the model can sound smart while remaining blind to consequence.

By work graph, I mean the relationships between people, goals, decisions, blockers, dependencies, commitments, artifacts, permissions, risks, policies, historical outcomes, confidence levels, blast radius, and feedback loops. Some of this can be represented explicitly. Some of it may need to be inferred. Some of it should probably remain under human control.

Without that graph, the model can summarize a thread without knowing which decision matters. It can draft an update without knowing what tradeoff is unresolved. It can close a ticket without knowing that the same issue will reappear next week. It can approve work without seeing the precedent being created.

I suspect this is one reason many AI features feel impressive in the moment but do not always change the system. The model has language context, but not enough consequence context. It sees the text around the task, but not the graph of obligations, risks, dependencies, and feedback that gives the task meaning.

Simulation is not prediction

The word "simulate" can sound more confident than I intend. I do not think the point is that a model can predict the future. It cannot. Work is too social, too contingent, too dependent on incentives, trust, timing, and incomplete information.

The point is more modest: the model can make possible consequences explicit enough for better human judgment.

That kind of simulation is structured reasoning. If we choose Path A, who benefits? Who is blocked? What risk increases? What precedent do we set? What future work do we create? What trust do we build or damage? What will this make easier or harder six months from now?

First-degree consequences are the immediate visible results. The launch ships. The customer receives the exception. The expense gets approved. The ticket closes. The meeting is scheduled.

Second-degree consequences are downstream behavior, precedent, risk, trust, coordination cost, future maintenance burden, or strategic drift. The launch increases support load. The exception becomes an expectation. The approval pattern teaches people the policy does not matter. The closed ticket hides a recurring product issue.

A useful harness would not pretend to know which path is correct. It would help the human see the decision with more of the consequence surface exposed.

Product launch: the decision is the work

Consider a product launch. A team is deciding whether to launch broadly, launch to beta users, or delay.

A Q&A machine can summarize bugs, internal threads, customer feedback, support notes, and launch criteria. That is useful because it reduces the cost of getting oriented.

An agentic assistant can draft the launch email, create the checklist, update the status page, and file follow-up tickets. Also useful.

A high-leverage harness would focus on the actual decision. What are the paths? What tradeoff is being made between speed and trust? What is the expected support load? Which commitments are affected? What quality risk are we accepting? Which customers or teams will be surprised by each path?

After human approval, the system could propagate the decision into launch plans, support guidance, roadmap notes, customer messaging, and follow-ups. Later, it could track whether the decision produced the expected outcome. Did support volume match the estimate? Did the beta path reduce risk? Did the delay actually improve quality, or did it mostly move uncertainty into the future?

The leverage is not that the system drafted a better launch email. The leverage is that it helped the team make and carry through the decision with better consequence awareness.

Customer escalation: the response is not the whole decision

Now imagine a customer escalation. A customer wants an exception.

A Q&A machine can summarize the customer history, agreement terms, past support issues, and previous commitments. An agentic assistant can draft a response, schedule a follow-up, and create an escalation ticket.

But the real judgment is not "write a response." It is whether to grant the exception, refuse it, or offer a temporary workaround. Granting the exception may preserve trust but create precedent. Refusing may protect the product boundary but damage the relationship. A workaround may buy time but add support burden or fragment the roadmap.

A high-leverage harness would compare those paths, surface the second-degree consequences, and ask the human to make the tradeoff-aware decision. It would then propagate the decision into the customer message, internal notes, follow-up owner, support guidance, and any policy or roadmap implication. Later, it would track whether the decision helped or created new cost.

The system should not hide the value judgment. Whose trust matters? Which precedent is acceptable? How much support burden is worth carrying? Those are human questions.

Expense approvals: repeated work may be a design smell

Expense approvals are a small example, but useful. Batch approving low-risk expenses saves time. A Q&A machine can explain the expense policy. An agentic assistant can approve obvious cases, reject incomplete ones, and ask for missing receipts.

But the high-leverage move may be different. Maybe the repeated approvals exist because the policy is unclear. Maybe people are asking for approval because they do not trust their interpretation. Maybe managers are approving the same category every week, which means the organization has accidentally turned policy ambiguity into recurring coordination labor.

A better harness might notice the pattern and ask a human whether the policy should change. If approved, it could help update the policy, communicate the change, adjust approval rules, and track whether future approval load decreases.

This shifts the work from processing repeated approvals to removing the cause of repeated approvals. That feels closer to leverage.

Cross-team misalignment: same word, different assumptions

Many coordination failures are semantic. Two teams use the same word but mean different things. Or they agree on a plan while carrying different assumptions about ownership, timing, API behavior, launch scope, or customer impact.

A Q&A machine can summarize what each team said. An agentic assistant can draft an alignment email. Both help.

A high-leverage harness would try to detect the mismatch itself. It might notice that one team is using "ready" to mean feature-complete, while another means support-trained and customer-safe. It might show likely consequences: duplicate work, incompatible APIs, launch delay, customer confusion, or a late change in ownership.

Then it would ask for a source-of-truth decision. Which definition are we using? Who owns the boundary? What needs to change in the doc, roadmap, tickets, and team follow-ups so the decision becomes real?

The leverage is not better summarization. It is catching the hidden ambiguity before the organization spends weeks executing different plans under the same label.

Hiring and resource allocation: maybe headcount is not the bottleneck

Resource planning is another place where the obvious task may not be the highest-leverage task. A team thinks it needs more headcount.

A Q&A machine can summarize the hiring plan, open roles, workload, and project commitments. An agentic assistant can draft a job description, create an interview plan, and schedule loops.

But maybe the bottleneck is not headcount. Maybe ownership is unclear. Maybe the team is blocked on a dependency. Maybe there are too many priorities. Maybe sequencing is poor. Maybe one decision from another group would unlock more than two new hires.

A high-leverage harness would help compare interventions: hiring, cutting scope, changing ownership, resolving a dependency, or resequencing work. It would not decide alone. But it could make the bottleneck question harder to skip.

That is the shift I keep noticing. The model can help create the job description. The harness should ask whether the job description is the right artifact to create.

The human moves from labor to judgment

The best harness may not remove humans from the loop. It may move humans to the highest-leverage point in the loop.

Current systems ask humans to do a lot of coordination labor. Read every thread. Remember context. Connect dots. Rewrite updates. Repeat decisions. Chase owners. Reconcile conflicting docs. Notice that two teams are misaligned. Remember what happened last time. Translate judgment into artifacts.

A neural harness should probably ask humans for different things: judgment, values, tradeoffs, taste, accountability, final calls, and exceptions. The machine can prepare the surface. The human should still own the decision where the decision matters.

This is a more humble view of automation than "the AI does the work." It says the AI should reduce the cost of reaching the right judgment point, not simply increase the amount of activity around the old one.

Failure modes

This idea has plenty of ways to go wrong.

The first failure mode is optimizing for measurable motion instead of net outcome. Emails sent, tickets closed, documents generated, and approvals processed are visible. Trust, quality, morale, future maintenance burden, and strategic drift are harder to measure. A bad harness will chase the visible thing.

The second is over-trusting simulated consequences. A model can make a path sound coherent without being right. The product has to keep simulation in the role of decision support, not prophecy.

The third is a bad or incomplete work graph. If the system does not understand the real dependencies, permissions, owners, risks, and history, it may surface the wrong judgment point with great confidence.

The fourth is permission collapse. A system that acts across tools can accidentally flatten boundaries that exist for good reasons. Access, approval, confidentiality, and blast radius are product constraints, not administrative details.

The fifth is invisible value judgment. "Net positive" depends on whose outcome matters. A decision can be good for speed and bad for quality, good for one team and costly for another, good this quarter and harmful later. The harness should not smuggle those values into the system invisibly.

The sixth is organizational politics. Surfacing leverage points may reveal uncomfortable bottlenecks. Maybe the issue is unclear ownership. Maybe a team is overloaded. Maybe the roadmap has too many priorities. Maybe a policy is incoherent. A useful system may make hidden tension visible, and not every organization will welcome that.

The shape is still unresolved

I do not think we know the final interface for this. It might not be a dashboard, inbox, canvas, graph, or chat. It may be some shape we have not invented yet.

I also do not think the old software objects disappear. Messages, docs, tickets, meetings, approvals, permissions, and audit trails still matter. In many cases they are exactly what make work safe enough to coordinate.

But I am starting to think that the most interesting AI products will not simply place a model inside every old workflow. They will ask what the model makes newly possible, and then design the harness around judgment, consequence, propagation, and learning.

The question changes from "How do we add AI to existing software?" to "What kind of harness does a neural computer actually need?"
