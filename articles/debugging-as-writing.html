Debugging as a writing exercise

How clearer notes can turn a messy investigation into a reusable playbook for the next confusing bug.

When a bug takes more than a few minutes, I usually need a written trail. Otherwise the investigation starts to loop: I retest the same theory, forget which boundary was checked, or lose the small clue that explained the failure.

The note does not need to be polished. It just needs to say what broke, what changed, what was tested, and what still looks suspicious.

Write the failure down

A failure becomes easier to reason about once it has a name, a symptom, a suspected boundary, and one falsifiable next check.

Keep the useful residue

The useful residue is the explanation that shortens the next investigation: the contract that mattered, the misleading symptom, and the check I would run first next time.
