SaaS Isn't Dead. Coding Was Never the Moat.

Agentic development compresses software creation, but it does not compress trust, distribution, customer understanding, or adoption.

Since agentic development became popular, I keep hearing a version of the same question:

If everyone can now vibe-code an app, why haven't we seen a flood of breakout new products?

If software can be generated so quickly, isn't SaaS dead?

My answer is: no.

But a certain kind of SaaS probably is.

Coding speed was never the whole moat

The mistake is assuming that the speed of coding was ever the main reason a product or business succeeded.

It wasn't.

Amazon did not win because it could write code faster than everyone else.

Microsoft did not win because it had the highest typing velocity.

Google did not win because the first version of the search box was hard to implement.

Code matters. Engineering quality matters. But the hardest parts of product development have usually lived outside the editor.

They live in questions like:

  • Who is the customer?
  • What problem is painful enough that they will change behavior?
  • How do we reach them?
  • Why should they trust us?
  • How does this fit into their workflow, team, budget, procurement process, compliance model, and emotional reality?
  • What makes them feel safe enough to adopt something new?

Building software and building a product are different jobs

Agentic development will make it dramatically easier to build software.

That is a huge shift.

But building software and building a product are not the same thing.

A product has to meet a customer. A product has to be adopted. A product has to survive contact with human fear, organizational inertia, budget constraints, IT policies, training gaps, and the simple fact that people are busy.

Customer analysis may become easier with AI. Prototyping may become easier. Iteration may become faster.

But you cannot vibe-code your way into customer acquisition.

You cannot generate trust with a prompt.

You cannot skip the messy human work of understanding why people do or do not change.

The same product lands differently in different segments

This becomes even more obvious when you look across customer segments.

A sophisticated enterprise customer with a strong IT department may be able to evaluate, deploy, secure, and integrate a new AI product relatively quickly.

A small business run out of a home office may experience the same product very differently. They may not have IT support. They may not know what permissions are safe. They may not understand what the agent is doing. They may worry about data, cost, control, or simply whether this thing will break their existing workflow.

In both cases, onboarding is not just a technical process.

It is a trust-building process.

The revolution still has to work for humans

That is why the argument that "AI will just eliminate humans from the process and make everything easier" feels fundamentally flawed to me.

Even if something is technically possible, society still has to accept it.

Customers have to accept it. Employees have to accept it. Managers, regulators, partners, and families have to accept it.

If the AI revolution is framed as replacing humans, removing agency, or forcing people into systems they do not trust, then society will resist it.

And it should.

The only version of this revolution that can actually scale is one that works for humans.

It has to create value for humans.

It has to give people leverage, not just anxiety.

It has to earn buy-in, not bypass it.

Product and Design matter more, not less

This is why I think Product and Design become more important in the agentic era, not less.

When code becomes cheaper, taste becomes more valuable.

When prototypes become abundant, judgment becomes more valuable.

When anyone can generate an app, the hard question shifts from "Can we build it?" to:

"Should this exist?"

"Who is it really for?"

"What human fear does it need to overcome?"

"What workflow does it need to respect?"

"What trust does it need to earn?"

"What does good actually look like?"

The supply side changed. The demand side did not disappear.

Agentic development changes the supply side of software. It makes creation faster, cheaper, and more accessible.

But it does not automatically solve the demand side.

It does not automatically create customer insight.

It does not automatically create distribution.

It does not automatically create trust.

It does not automatically make people want to change how they work.

SaaS is not dead. The shallow moat is.

So no, I don't think SaaS is dead.

But SaaS that only existed because software was hard to write may be in trouble.

The next generation of products will not win simply because they were coded faster.

They will win because they understand people better.

They will win because they reduce fear.

They will win because they fit into real workflows.

They will win because they combine agentic capability with human-centered product thinking.

In the agentic era, the bottleneck is not code.

The bottleneck is trust.

And that makes product development more human, not less.
